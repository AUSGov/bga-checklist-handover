1 Decide on the type of employee

Tip - Assess your business needs

Before you start, it’s a good idea to assess your business needs so you can choose the right person for your business. Think about:

  • the tasks you need the employee to do
  • any skills and qualifications they’ll require
  • how busy the job will be
  • how long you think you’ll need someone for

Decide on the employment type

Must do

Depending on the requirements of the job, you might want a:

  • full-time or part-time employee
  • fixed-term employee (non-ongoing)
  • casual employee
  • trainee or apprentice
  • contractor
  • labour-hire employee from an agency

The type of worker you decide on will affect your obligations.

Find out more about the different types of employee


Know the difference between an employee and contractor

Must do

This will affect your obligations. In general:

  • independent contractors work for themselves and are their own boss
  • employees work in someone else’s business – the business controls how, where and when they do their work, and pays them a wage

But you need to consider a range of factors when deciding whether someone is an employee or contractor. 

  • Check the differences between employees and contractors on the Australian Taxation Office website – this will help you to work out your business’s tax and super obligations.
  • Read the Fair Work Ombudsman’s independent contracting page to understand contractor entitlements.

2 Assess the impacts and costs

Tip – Know what you’re in for

Consider whether you can afford the potential costs and impacts on your business, including:

  • recruitment costs
  • wages
  • allowances, overtime and penalty rates
  • tax and super
  • workers compensation insurance
  • changes to your workplace to make it accessible and safe
  • changes to the way you operate your business

Find pay rates and conditions

Must do

For most employees, you can find the minimum pay rates and conditions in the relevant award. An award is a document that sets out the minimum pay and conditions for a particular industry or occupation. Use the Find my award tool to check:

  • pay
  • hours of work
  • rosters
  • breaks
  • allowances
  • penalty rates
  • overtime

You can also use the Pay Calculator to work out what you’ll need to pay your new employee under the relevant award (including any penalty rates).

While some employees aren’t covered by an award or agreement, all employees in Australia are entitled to:

  • a minimum wage
  • 10 minimum entitlements that include maximum working hours, leave entitlements and termination requirements (10 National Employment Standards (NES))

You must meet your obligations under the relevant award and the NES. If you don’t, you may face penalties.

If you’re considering a job trial or probation period, you’ll still need to check entitlements for pay and conditions.

If you’re in WA, check for pay rates and conditions under the WA industrial relations system.


Understand potential impacts on your workplace

Must do

When you take on an employee, you’re responsible for managing them and providing them with a healthy and safe working environment. To do this, you may have to make changes to your workplace. Make sure you provide:

  • safe premises
  • safe machinery and materials
  • safe systems of work
  • information, instruction, training and supervision
  • a suitable working environment and facilities

You will also need to have worker’s compensation insurance for your employee.

If you want to learn more, check our Managing and developing staff topic.


3 Recruit

Tip – Advertise and select an employee

To get the right person for your business, take time to:

  • prepare your job description
  • advertise the position
  • evaluate applications and interview
  • make an offer

Follow our steps to recruiting

Find handy recruitment templates (including a job description, job advertising and letter of engagement template)

Don't discriminate

Must do

Discrimination in the workplace is illegal. Make sure there is no discrimination during your recruitment process or when an employee is working for you.

You must not discriminate against job applicants or employees because of their:

  • race
  • colour
  • sex
  • sexual preference
  • age
  • physical or mental disability
  • marital status
  • family or carer’s responsibilities
  • pregnancy
  • religion
  • political opinion
  • national extraction
  • social origin

Read more about discrimination and how to get help with discrimination issues

If you employ someone with a disability, you may need to adjust your workplace to ensure it's accessible. Most people with a disability won't need changes to the workplace, so it's a good idea to chat with them first.


Check the employee can work in Australia

Must do

Once you’ve found someone, you’re responsible for making sure they can legally work in Australia. This includes employees you get from a contractor or labour hire company.

If you’re considering hiring a worker from overseas, you must make sure they have the correct type of visa. To check if their visa allows them to work in Australia, you can use the free online service Visa Entitlement Verification Online (VEVO).

Migrant workers and visa holders, including international students, have the same workplace rights as other workers in Australia.

Find out how to check if someone can legally work in Australia and access VEVO.


Check licences and qualifications

Must do

Depending on your business type, your worker may need specific licences or qualifications to do their job. Check they have the correct licences and, if they don’t, consider providing training.

Check your industry licence and permit requirements.

Search the Australian Business Licence and Information Service to find licences you may need.


4 Prepare for your new employee

Tip – Induct your new employee

Investing time in a thorough induction will help you get the most from your new employee. An induction helps them get to know your business, your workplace and their role. This is a good time to provide them with things like tax and super forms, their employment conditions and your work health and safety information.

Check this list of what Australian businesses should include in a staff induction.

Provide your employee with information on their entitlements

Must do

You must provide your new employee with the Fair Work Information Statement (FWIS) before, or as soon as possible after, they start working for you. This provides them with information about their conditions of employment, including the National Employment Standards. 

Download a copy of the Fair Work Information Statement  to provide to your employee. 

If your employee is covered by an award, you must make the  relevant award  easily available to them. You may provide a digital version or a hard copy (e.g. in a prominent place in the tea room or on a noticeboard at work).  


Agree to hours and rostering

Must do

Under most awards you and your employee must agree on the hours of work and rostering in advance. If you’re taking on a casual worker, check the award first. They may be entitled to a minimum number of hours.

Download templates to help you prepare your roster and manage work hours.


Prepare for tax and super payments

Must do

Register for pay as you go (PAYG) withholding – You need to withhold tax from payments to your employee in most cases. You’ll collect these PAYG withholding amounts and send them to the Australian Taxation Office (ATO) at regular intervals.

Before you can do this, you must register for PAYG withholding.

Check whether you need to withhold tax from your worker.

Tax file number (TFN) declaration – Get your new employee to complete a tax file number (TFN) declaration form. The information they provide in the form will help you to work out how much tax you need to take out of their pay.

Read more about completing a TFN declaration

Superannuation standard choice form – You’ll need to pay to a super fund for eligible employees and some contractors. This is called the super guarantee and it applies to full-time, part-time, and casual employees.

If your employee is entitled to the super guarantee, check if they're eligible to choose a super fund. If so, give them a Standard choice form within 28 days of them starting.

Check the relevant award to see if there are any other superannuation obligations that you may need to comply with.

Provide their TFN to their super fund the next time you make a payment for them (or within 14 days, whichever is later).


5 Pay your employee the right amount

Find the relevant award

Must do

Your employee's minimum wages, including penalty rates and overtime, will come from the relevant award. Some employees have a different minimum wage in the award, for example:

  • juniors
  • apprentices and trainees

Use the Pay and Conditions Tool (PACT) to calculate pay rates from the award.

If your employee isn’t covered by an award, check the national minimum wage.


Withhold tax from payments

Must do

You must collect pay as you go (PAYG) tax from employee payments. Work out the amount to withhold from payments to your employee using the tax withheld calculator or tax tables.


Provide payslips

Must do

Each pay period you must give your employee a payslip within 1 working day of pay day showing the amount paid and the amount withheld.

Check what to include on a pay slip and download a payslip template


Keep up to date with pay rates

Must do

Make sure you stay on top of any changes to awards or pay rates. Examples of when these can change include when:

  • awards and the national minimum wage are reviewed each year – any changes usually take effect around 1 July
  • a junior employee has their birthday
  • an apprentice reaches a milestone in their apprenticeship
  • your employee’s role changes

Subscribe to email updates to find out when awards change


6 Pay superannuation and tax

Pay super

Must do

Each quarter, you’ll need to:

  • work out how much super to pay your employee
  • report and pay super contributions electronically using the SuperStream standard by the due date

Pay fringe benefits tax – if required

Must do

Fringe benefits tax (FBT) is a tax you need to pay if you provide certain benefits to your employee outside of their salary. This includes things like:

  • letting them use a work car to drive between work and home
  • car parking

If you provide any fringe benefits to your employees, you need to be registered for FBT.


Check if you need to pay payroll tax

Must do

If the total amount of wages you pay exceeds a certain amount, you may have to pay a state or territory payroll tax.


7 Keep necessary records

Employment records and payslips

Must do

As an employer, you must make and keep employment and financial records.

You need to keep employee records for 7 years, including records about their:

  • employment
  • pay
  • hours
  • leave
  • super

Read more about keeping employee records and find handy templates to help manage them.


Records for tax and super

Must do

You must keep all records for your employee relating to tax and superannuation for 5 years.

The ATO website has information to help you understand which tax and super records you need to keep for your worker.


8 Report payments, tax and super

Report payments to the ATO

Must do

You need to report payments made to your employees to the ATO, including:

  • salaries and wages
  • PAYG withholding
  • super

Learn more about reporting tax and super and when to report.


Check if you need Single Touch Payroll (STP)

Must do

STP is a new way of reporting tax and superannuation information to the ATO. With STP you report your payroll information each time you pay your employee through STP-enabled software.

STP started from 1 July 2019 for small businesses with less than 20 employees. But if you employ:

  • 4 people or less – you have until 30 June 2021 to move to STP. Until then, you have the option of asking your tax agent to report for you using STP
  • family (or other ‘closely held payees’) – you have until 1 July 2020 to start STP reporting

Find out more about Single Touch Payroll.


9 Know the rules around ending employment

Understand how to end employment

Must do

However someone’s employment ends, it’s important you follow the rules about dismissal, notice and final pay. 

If things don’t work out with your employee, you’ll need to meet termination requirements under the NES and relevant award (such as providing the right amount of notice and final pay).

If you're a small business with less than 15 employees, an employee needs to have been working for you for at least 12 months before they can make a claim for unfair dismissal.

Learn more about ending employment


Final payments

Must do

You’ll need to work out if any part of their final payment is an employment termination payment (ETP). These are lump sum payments that are taxed at concessional rates depending on your employee's age and length of employment.

Work out the PAYG separately for the ETP part of the final payment. Final super and non-ETP payments are worked out as usual.

Check the ATO website for more on what to do when an employee leaves.
